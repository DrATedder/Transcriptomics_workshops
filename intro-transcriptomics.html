Pre-Session Reading: Introduction to Transcriptomics

Goal: Equip students with foundational knowledge of transcriptomics, sequencing technologies, data processing pipelines, and research applications.

1. Introduction to Transcriptomics

Transcriptomics is the study of the complete set of RNA transcripts produced by the genome under specific circumstances or in a specific cell. It provides a dynamic view of gene activity, as opposed to genomics, which offers a static snapshot of potential function. Through transcriptomic analyses, researchers can investigate gene expression changes in response to environmental stimuli, disease states, developmental stages, or treatment conditions.

This field is crucial in understanding functional genomics and offers insight into biological mechanisms at the molecular level. It enables researchers to identify biomarkers, characterize disease pathways, and infer gene regulation networks.

2. Sequencing Methods in Transcriptomics

Short-Read RNA-Seq (Illumina)

Short-read sequencing technologies, especially Illumina-based RNA-Seq, dominate the field. These methods generate millions of reads of 50–300 bp length, enabling high-throughput analysis of gene expression. Advantages include high accuracy, scalability, and established bioinformatic pipelines. However, limitations include poor resolution of full-length transcripts, difficulties in isoform detection, and biases introduced during amplification and alignment.
Stengths: high accuracy, deep coverage
Limitations: difficulty resolving isoforms and repetitive regions
Reference: Wang et al. (2009), Nat Rev Genet, DOI:10.1038/nrg2484

Long-Read Sequencing (PacBio, Oxford Nanopore)

Long-read technologies provide full-length transcript information, improving isoform discovery and structural variant detection. PacBio’s Iso-Seq and Oxford Nanopore platforms offer read lengths in the thousands of base pairs. These methods are valuable for resolving complex transcriptomes but often have higher error rates and lower throughput compared to short-read sequencing.
Stengths: full-length transcripts, better isoform annotation
Limitations: potentially higher error rate, lower throughput, more expensive
Reference: Byrne et al. (2017), Nat Commun, DOI:10.1038/ncomms16027

Microarrays

Although less commonly used today, microarrays are hybridization-based platforms for detecting known transcripts. They rely on complementary probes and are cost-effective but limited to pre-defined transcript sets and have lower dynamic range and specificity.
Stengths: high accuracy and cost effective
Limitations: limited to pre-defined transcript sets and have lower dynamic range and specificity.
Reference: Allison et al. (2006), Nat Rev Genet, DOI:10.1038/nrg1749

Single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq)

scRNA-seq enables transcriptomic profiling at the individual cell level. It is useful in identifying cell types, studying differentiation, and understanding heterogeneity in tissues or tumors.
Challenges: low input RNA, high noise, and data sparsity
Reference: Tang F et al. (2009) Nat Methods, DOI: 10.1038/nmeth.1315

4. Types of Questions Addressed by Transcriptomics

Which genes are differentially expressed?

This is the most common goal: identifying genes that change significantly across conditions. For example, which genes are upregulated in tumor tissue vs. healthy controls? These are identified using statistical models that account for variability, such as DESeq2 or Limma-Voom.

How do pathways or processes change?

Instead of individual genes, we can test for enrichment of entire biological pathways. Tools like DAVID or g:Profiler map gene sets to functional annotations.

What isoforms are expressed?

Using long-read or isoform-aware tools, transcriptomics can reveal alternative splicing patterns or isoform usage, which may be crucial in disease mechanisms.

How do samples cluster?

PCA, t-SNE, or hierarchical clustering allow researchers to assess similarities between samples, detect outliers, and explore batch effects or hidden structure.

5. Limitations and Considerations

Sample Quality

RNA integrity is crucial. Degraded RNA leads to poor sequencing results and biases in gene expression estimation. Always perform RIN scoring or electrophoresis before library prep.
Reference: Fleige & Pfaffl. (2006) DOI:https:10.1016/j.mam.2005.12.003

Batch Effects

Technical variation from different runs, reagent lots, or sequencing machines can introduce batch effects. These can confound biological signals and must be corrected (e.g., via ComBat).
Reference: Leek et al. (2010) DOI:10.1038/nrg2825

Sequencing Depth

Too few reads per sample limits sensitivity. Gene discovery and quantification require appropriate sequencing depth (e.g., 30M reads/sample for human bulk RNA-Seq).
Reference: Roobinson & Storey. (2014) DOI:10.1093/bioinformatics/btu552

Normalization

Read counts must be normalized to control for library size and composition bias (e.g., TMM, DESeq2's size factor). Improper normalization can inflate false positives or obscure true biological effects.
Reference: Bullard et al. (2010) DOI:10.1186/1471-2105-11-94

Multiple Testing

Thousands of genes are tested in parallel, requiring control for false discovery. Standard practice uses Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction with adj.P.Val < 0.05.
Reference: Benjamini & Hochberg. (1995) DOI:10.1111/j.2517-6161.1995.tb02031.x
